What are X-Ray tubes?

There are several modalities in radiology that use X-ray tubes, such as Radiology, Fluoroscopy, Mammography, and Computed Tomography.

In all cases, the function and components of the X-Ray tube is widely similar. The fundemental components of an X-Ray tube are its cathode, anode, glass envelope, and tube housing.

What do they do?

The cathode is typically a small tungsten filament similar to that of an incandescent light bulb, it creates free electrons through thermionic emissions. Thermionic emission (a.k.a. the Edison effect) happens when the metal gets so hot that the thermal vibrational energy overcome the electrostatic forces holding electrons to the surface. When current flows throught the metal, the metal heats up. The hot metal is now surrounded by a "cloud" of electrons, which are 'boiling' off the metal surface and falling back again. This is what is called a "space charge effect".
The more current flowing through the metal, the hotter it gets. However, this only works up to 1000mA. #why?

Electrons get absorbed at the anode, and this is where X-rays are produced. There are two kinds of anodes: rotating and stationary.
Stationary anodes have a simple design, low heat capacity, and has a low exposure. While a rotating anode with a long focal track spreads out electrons over a larger area. The complex design has a higher heat capacity and higher exposure.
As the higher exposure of the rotating anode gives higher quality imaging, it typically lends itself to being used in X-ray tubes.
